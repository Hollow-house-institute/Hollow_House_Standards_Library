The Execution-Time Governance Gap: Decision Boundaries, Interaction Traces, and Runtime Accountability in AI Systems

Abstract

AI governance expanded rapidly across regulatory, standards, and enterprise environments. Oversight obligations now exist across accountability, transparency, intervention capability, auditability, and operational risk management. Runtime systems evolved faster than the governance structures intended to constrain them.

Policies exist. Operational systems continue executing without enforceable Decision Boundaries, attributable Governance Telemetry, or auditable Interaction Traces.

This creates an execution-time governance gap: a separation between governance obligations and runtime enforcement capability. Oversight becomes procedural rather than operational. Accountability diffuses across organizational and technical layers. Intervention authority weakens under automation scale and workflow compression.

This paper examines that gap and introduces an execution-time governance model centered on:

Decision Boundary enforcement

Governance Telemetry

Stop Authority

Escalation controls

Interaction Trace continuity

Continuous Assurance

Longitudinal Accountability


The objective is not additional governance documentation. The objective is runtime governance infrastructure capable of producing attributable evidence during execution.

1. Governance Failure at Runtime

AI governance currently operates with a structural mismatch.

Governance obligations are frequently defined outside the runtime systems expected to comply with them. Policies describe acceptable behavior while operational systems continue executing through orchestration pipelines, automation layers, probabilistic outputs, workflow engines, APIs, and distributed approval structures that often lack governance continuity during execution.

The governance layer becomes observational instead of enforceable.

Several operational patterns emerge repeatedly:

intervention authority exists formally but weakens operationally

escalation pathways degrade under runtime pressure

human oversight collapses into procedural approval

audit systems capture technical events while omitting governance state

accountability disperses across vendors, operators, deployment systems, and organizational layers


The issue is not absence of governance activity.

The issue is governance discontinuity during runtime execution.

Operational systems can remain technically stable while governance integrity deteriorates simultaneously.

That distinction matters.

A technically functional system can still produce:

Governance Drift

Accountability Diffusion

Decision Substitution

Override Erosion

escalation failure

intervention latency

authority ambiguity


Current governance approaches frequently identify risk categories while failing to preserve runtime accountability continuity.

The result is Post-Hoc Governance.

Evidence is reconstructed after operational failure rather than generated continuously during execution.

2. The Execution-Time Governance Gap

The execution-time governance gap emerges when governance obligations exist independently from runtime enforcement capability.

Standards bodies and regulatory systems consistently require:

accountability

oversight

transparency

intervention capability

monitoring

traceability


Operational systems frequently lack the infrastructure necessary to maintain those conditions continuously during execution.

Several recurring failure patterns emerge from that separation.

Governance Drift

Governance controls weaken over time as operational systems evolve faster than oversight structures.

Runtime environments change continuously:

deployment architectures shift

orchestration layers expand

automation increases

workflows compress

operators rotate

intervention frequency declines


Governance assumptions remain comparatively static.

The operational system changes. Governance continuity weakens.

Accountability Diffusion

Responsibility disperses across:

model providers

deployment teams

operators

governance personnel

workflow automation

integrated vendor systems


without persistent attribution linking authority to operational action.

Organizations often possess fragmented technical logs while lacking attributable governance evidence explaining:

who acted

under what authority

under which Decision Boundary

with what escalation condition

with what intervention rationale


Actions become visible. Accountability continuity does not.

Decision Substitution

Decision Substitution occurs when operational systems begin functioning beyond their intended authority scope because Decision Boundaries are undefined, ambiguous, or unenforced during execution.

This condition typically emerges gradually.

Operators initially supervise outputs actively. Over time:

trust accumulates

escalation becomes informal

intervention frequency decreases

approval compresses into procedural confirmation


Operational authority migrates from governed oversight into workflow momentum.

Override Erosion

Operators may technically retain authority while lacking practical intervention capability.

Several conditions contribute:

automation dependency

approval fatigue

escalation ambiguity

compressed operational timelines

excessive trust in system stability

unclear accountability ownership


The override mechanism exists procedurally while degrading operationally.

Post-Hoc Governance

Governance evidence is frequently generated retrospectively after incidents, investigations, or audit requests.

That structure creates delayed accountability.

Execution-time governance requires evidence continuity during runtime operation rather than after operational failure.

3. Runtime Governance Primitives

Execution-time governance depends on operational primitives capable of preserving governance continuity during live execution.

These primitives function as runtime governance infrastructure rather than descriptive policy layers.

Decision Boundary

A Decision Boundary defines:

who may act

under what authority

under what operational conditions

within what intervention limits

under what escalation requirements


Decision Boundaries must remain observable during execution.

Static governance documentation is insufficient if operational systems cannot enforce authority conditions during runtime activity.

Execution-time governance treats Decision Boundaries as active operational constraints.

Governance Telemetry

Governance Telemetry captures governance-relevant operational evidence including:

actor identity

operational action

timestamp

escalation state

authority condition

intervention outcome

rationale

Decision Boundary reference


Traditional technical telemetry captures infrastructure state.

Governance Telemetry captures accountability state.

Without attributable governance telemetry:

escalation continuity weakens

intervention traceability disappears

Longitudinal Accountability becomes unstable

audit survivability deteriorates


Stop Authority

Stop Authority provides operational capability to interrupt, suspend, override, or halt execution when governance thresholds are breached.

Stop Authority cannot remain symbolic.

A governance system that cannot operationally interrupt execution during threshold breach conditions does not possess meaningful runtime authority.

Stop Authority also requires:

intervention visibility

attributable invocation

escalation continuity

timestamp persistence

rationale capture


Without those conditions, intervention evidence degrades rapidly.

Escalation

Escalation defines mandatory intervention pathways triggered by:

operational anomalies

governance threshold breaches

authority conflicts

behavioral instability

override conditions

unresolved intervention states


Escalation structures preserve intervention continuity when routine operational controls become insufficient.

Execution-time governance treats escalation as an operational enforcement mechanism rather than a procedural notification layer.

Interaction Trace

Interaction Traces preserve governance continuity across:

actions

interventions

authority transitions

escalation sequences

override events

operational handoffs


Technical logs frequently omit governance context.

Interaction Traces preserve:

why action occurred

under whose authority

under what Decision Boundary

with what intervention conditions

with what escalation state


This creates attributable governance continuity across runtime operation.

Continuous Assurance

Continuous Assurance replaces periodic governance review with persistent runtime verification.

Governance integrity cannot depend exclusively on quarterly audits, retrospective reviews, or static documentation updates.

Execution-time governance requires continuous operational visibility into:

intervention behavior

escalation frequency

authority continuity

override conditions

governance stability

Decision Boundary adherence


Governance becomes measurable longitudinally rather than episodically.
